Aerolith is a project I'm building with a friend: a custom cinelifter drone
designed to carry a mirrorless camera (A7s III class) for cinematic aerial
footage. The goal was never to buy a ready-made solution, the goal was to
design, build, and fly something entirely our own.

This post is a status update on where we are.

---

## Why From Scratch?

There are excellent commercial cinelifters on the market. We chose not to use
them because we wanted to understand and own every layer of the system.
Custom electronics force you to think about power stages, analog signal chains,
MCU, sensor interfaces and communication protocols.

---

## ESC

The ESC design started on a lighter predecessor called Nanolith. Those ESCs,
rated up to 3S, are now fully validated: motors spin, respond correctly to
DSHOT commands, and behave exactly as expected under load. Satisfying to see
after a lot of schematic iterations and PCB work.

For Aerolith, we are redesigning the ESC for 6S operation. This means
significantly larger MOSFETs to handle the higher currents, and a reworked
analog signal chain voltage dividers, gate drivers, bootstrap circuits  
adapted to operate correctly at higher bus voltages. The architecture is
largely the same but component choices and tolerances are completely different.

---

## Flight Controller

The FC is built around an STM32F405, paired with an ICM-42688 IMU and an ST
barometer. We designed the schematic, laid out the PCB, ordered
boards and assembled them ourselves in at the INSA Fablab.

Hardware bring-up was kind of humbling. We hit two (very dumb) bugs that took
longer than expected to track down.

**Boot pin floating.** The DFU boot button GND floating, so USB
DFU mode simply did not trigger. SWD worked fine over the debug header, which
is how we eventually figured it out.

**VDDA disconnected from 3V3.** The analog supply for the MCU was not connected to
the digital 3V3 rail. Once we connected VDDA by bridging over a capacitor, everything worked.

The IMU gave us a separate headache. The ICM-42688 is almost impossible to
source right now, largely due to supply chain disruption from the war in
Ukraine. We used a Chinese clone that looks physically identical but has
different register maps and initialization sequences. We spent a long time
convinced our hot-air soldering was the problem the joints were actually
fine. The driver simply needed to be written from scratch against the clone's
real datasheet.

The board now runs firmware reliably over both USB and SWD. We are currently
developing the ELRS library to interface an off-the-shelf ELRS module for the
radio link.

---

## Frame

The frame is designed in Fusion 360 and intended for fabrication at the INSA
fablab using laser cutting: two main plates and arms that slot into integrated
interfaces, assembled with standoffs and screws. Efficient and easy to
disassemble for maintenance. We manufactured V1 using acrylic panels to iterate fast and cut costs.


V1 was 330mm motor-to-motor. After reviewing propeller geometry and airflow, we
realized the spacing was tight enough to cause turbulence between
rotors. V2 stretches to 350mm to address this before we cut the first real
carbon plates.

---

## Firmware

Fully written from scratch on FreeRTOS. We have a working base of low-level
drivers, including a working ICM driver. The overall firmware architecture is being finalized before we start the
control layer.
